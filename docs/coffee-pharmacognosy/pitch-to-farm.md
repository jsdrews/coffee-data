# What's In It for Nogales?

Why letting this research happen at the farm is a good deal — from the farm's perspective.

## It costs the farm almost nothing

- She's not asking for a budget. Equipment is ~$100–200 (pH meter, thermometer, swabs). Lab costs (~$800–1600 for sequencing) are on her.
- She's not asking for separate cherry. She works with the same lots the farm is already processing. The experiments happen alongside normal operations — same cherry, same tanks, same drying beds.
- She's not slowing anything down. The experimental fermentations run in parallel with the farm's regular production. pH and temperature logging adds ~15 minutes per lot per day.
- The cherry isn't wasted. Every experimental lot gets dried, roasted, and cupped as usual. The coffee is still sellable. She's just measuring more carefully along the way.

## What the farm gets for free

### Immediate: better data about your own process

Most specialty farms operate on intuition and recipes. "Ferment 72 hours, dry 14 days, roast to first crack + 1:30." It works, but you don't know *why* it works, which means you can't fix it when it doesn't.

She's building a structured data system that tracks every lot from cherry to cup:
- **Fermentation curves** (pH, temperature, Brix over time) — not just duration but what's actually happening chemically
- **Variety × process comparisons** — does your Bourbon score higher washed or natural? You'll have data, not guesses
- **Lot-to-lot consistency tracking** — are your fermentations producing the same curves every time, or is there variation you're not seeing?

This data system stays at the farm after she leaves. The templates, protocols, and tracking sheets are yours.

### Immediate: direct answers to questions you're already asking

Every farm wonders:
- "Should I process this variety washed or natural?" → She runs the split and cups both
- "Is my fermentation consistent across lots?" → The pH curves tell you
- "Do my different parcels produce different results?" → She compares them with identical processing
- "Am I drying too fast? Too slow?" → Daily moisture logs reveal the pattern

These aren't abstract research questions. They're operational questions the farm already has, answered with data instead of feel.

### Medium-term: your farm's microbial fingerprint

If the microbial sampling happens, Nogales gets something almost no specialty farm in the world has: a map of which bacteria and yeasts are present in your fermentations.

This tells you:
- **Whether your fermentation is dominated by desirable microbes** (Leuconostoc, Lactobacillus, Pichia) or undesirable ones
- **Whether different varieties attract different microbes** at your farm — which could explain why the same process produces different cup profiles
- **Whether your different parcels seed different microbial communities** — soil microbes transfer to the cherry surface, so different soil = different starting microbes

This is the foundation for moving from "ferment for 72 hours" to "ferment until the microbial community reaches this state." That's process control, not recipes.

### Longer-term: marketing and competitive positioning

**Data-backed traceability.** Buyers increasingly want more than "washed Geisha, 1800m, Huila." A farm that can say "this lot was fermented under controlled conditions, with pH-monitored fermentation, and here's the microbial community profile that produced these flavor characteristics" is selling a different product. That commands premium pricing.

**If this gets published, the farm gets named.** Research papers name their study sites. Farms like Finca El Injerto (Guatemala) and Hacienda La Esmeralda (Panama) built part of their global brand through association with research and innovation. A published paper on variety-specific microbiome profiles at Nogales = permanent marketing in the specialty market.

**Functional coffee positioning.** If compound tracking happens (either now or in a follow-up), the farm could have data showing specific bioactive compound levels in their coffee. "Our washed Geisha retains 60% of chlorogenic acid content through processing" is a health-positioning story that opens markets beyond cup score.

## What it doesn't cost

- **No loss of production.** Every experimental lot is still sellable coffee.
- **No disruption to normal operations.** She works around the farm's schedule, not the other way around.
- **No IP risk.** The data is about coffee science, not the farm's trade secrets. If anything, published results increase the farm's visibility and reputation.
- **No ongoing obligation.** If she leaves after 6 weeks, the farm has protocols and data. If she stays through harvest, the farm has more. Either way, the farm is better off than before.

## What the farm is really being asked

Let someone who is motivated, educated, and willing to work for free:
1. Measure things about your process that you'd want to know anyway
2. Build a data system you can keep using
3. Put your farm's name in the scientific literature

The farm provides access and cherry (which it's processing anyway). She provides the labor, the equipment, the lab costs, and the expertise.

## Comparable examples

Specialty farms that have benefited from hosting research:

- **Hacienda La Esmeralda (Panama)** — Geisha variety research helped establish them as the most prestigious farm in specialty coffee. Their auction lots regularly sell for $1,000+/lb.
- **Finca El Injerto (Guatemala)** — Research collaboration on variety trials and processing innovation contributed to their reputation and consistent top-10 Cup of Excellence finishes.
- **Daterra (Brazil)** — Their data-driven approach to lot tracking and variety management (which started with research partnerships) is now central to their brand story.
- **Various farms in the Nariño/Huila corridor** — Cenicafé research partnerships have helped farms optimize variety selection for their specific microclimates.

None of these farms regret hosting researchers. The research output becomes part of the farm's story.

## The ask, concretely

**Option A (6 weeks, mid-May departure):**
- Access to cherry from 2–3 varieties during harvest
- Permission to monitor fermentation (pH, temp) on experimental and regular lots
- Use of existing processing infrastructure (tanks, drying beds, Roest)
- 15 minutes of someone's time per lot per day for measurements
- Space to store sampling supplies

**Option B (through full harvest, ~3 months):**
- Same as above, extended through June
- Ideally, some coordination on which varieties get processed when (to enable controlled comparisons)
- Cupping sessions with the farm team (benefits both sides — calibration)

In return: structured data, written protocols, variety/process comparison results, and potentially the farm's name in a scientific publication.
