# Six-Week Plan: April 3 – Mid-May 2026

What can be accomplished at Nogales in ~6 weeks during the mitaca (secondary harvest) in Bruselas, Huila.

## The key insight

The 6 weeks are for **collecting data and samples**. The analysis, sequencing results, and publications happen in the months after she leaves. The farm keeps the data infrastructure. She keeps the dataset and the research narrative. Both benefit long after May.

## Critical first question: what's being harvested?

Before anything else, she needs to understand:
- **Which varieties** are producing ripe cherry right now? (Mitaca is smaller than the main Oct–Dec harvest — not everything will be ready)
- **How many parcels** does Nogales have, at what elevations, with what varieties?
- **What's the farm's current process?** How do they ferment, dry, roast, cup? What do they already track?

This determines the experimental design. If only Caturra and Bourbon are ripe, the variety comparison is Caturra vs. Bourbon. If Geisha is also available, even better. Work with what the harvest gives you.

## Week-by-week plan

### Week 1 (April 3–9): Setup

**At the farm:**
- Map what's available: which varieties, which parcels, what's ripe or nearly ripe
- Understand the farm's existing workflow and what they already record
- Inventory equipment: do they have a pH meter? Refractometer? If not, order from Bogotá (next-day delivery to Neiva, then transport to Bruselas)
- Set up the lot tracking template (spreadsheet or notebook) with fields for every pipeline stage

**Off-farm:**
- Contact lab partners for microbial sequencing:
  - **Cenicafé** (Chinchiná, Caldas) — Colombia's coffee research institute, most relevant
  - **Universidad Nacional** (Bogotá or Medellín) — microbiology or food science departments
  - **Universidad Surcolombiana** (Neiva) — closest university to Bruselas
  - Ask: can they process 16S/ITS samples? What's the cost and turnaround? What sample format do they need?
- Order sterile sampling supplies if needed: swabs, 15ml tubes, labels, cooler for cold chain. Basic microbiology suppliers in Bogotá can ship these.

**Deliverable:** Experimental design document — which varieties, how many lots, what comparisons, what measurements at each stage. This is a 1-page plan that the rest of the weeks execute.

### Week 2 (April 10–16): First fermentations

**If cherry is available** (mitaca timing depends on the specific parcels):

**Experiment 1 — Variety comparison (Hypothesis 1):**
- Harvest 2–3 varieties on the same day (or as close as possible)
- Process all with identical washed anaerobic fermentation
- At each fermentation: log pH, temperature, and Brix every 4–6 hours
- If sampling supplies are ready: collect swabs/liquid at 0h, 24h, 48h, and end of fermentation
- Label everything: variety, parcel, date, timepoint

**Experiment 2 — Process comparison:**
- Take one variety and split into 3 lots: washed, honey, natural
- Same measurements as above
- This directly answers: "does this variety score higher washed or natural?" with data

**Ongoing:**
- Begin structured lot tracking for ALL lots the farm processes, not just experiments
- This is the data infrastructure that persists after she leaves

### Week 3 (April 17–23): Drying phase + second round

- First fermentations are now drying — log daily: moisture content, ambient temperature, humidity, bean temperature, weight
- If more cherry is coming ripe: run a second round of fermentations (replication strengthens the data)
- If the farm has parcels at different elevations or soil types and the same variety is ripe at both: run **Experiment 3 — Terroir comparison** (same variety, different parcels, identical processing)
- Continue structured lot tracking

### Week 4 (April 24–30): Roasting begins

- First dried lots should be reaching target moisture (~10–12%) by now (drying takes 10–18 days depending on method and weather)
- Roast on the Roest with full profile logging
- Try 2–3 roast profiles on the same green to see how roast level affects the cup
- Link roast data back to the lot: cherry Brix → fermentation curve → drying profile → roast profile
- Continue drying/fermenting later lots

### Week 5 (May 1–7): Cupping and analysis

- Cup all completed lots using full SCA protocol
- Ideally cup with 2+ people for calibration (the farm team, visiting cuppers, etc.)
- Now the full pipeline is connected for at least some lots: soil → cherry → fermentation → drying → green → roast → cup
- Start compiling the data: what patterns emerge?
  - Did the two varieties cup differently? How?
  - Did washed vs. natural vs. honey produce different scores? Different cupping attribute profiles?
  - Do the fermentation curves (pH, temp) look different between varieties?

### Week 6 (May 8–15): Documentation and handoff

**For the farm:**
- Clean, compiled dataset of every lot tracked during her stay
- Written protocols: how to log fermentation (what to measure, when, in what format), how to track lots, how to link roast profiles to processing data
- Templates the farm team can continue using — the data collection doesn't stop when she leaves
- Preliminary findings: "here's what we saw in 6 weeks, here's what it suggests, here's what to watch for next main harvest"

**For the research:**
- Ship any collected microbial samples to the lab (if not already done)
- Compile raw data into structured CSV/spreadsheets
- Write a research summary: experimental design, methods, preliminary observations
- Identify what to do with the data once sequencing results come back (this is months of follow-up work)

**For relationships:**
- Thank the farm team — they made this possible
- Establish a plan for staying in contact and sharing results
- If a lab partnership formed, clarify next steps and timelines for sample processing

## What she leaves with

### For Nogales

| Deliverable | Value |
|-------------|-------|
| Structured lot tracking data (every lot during her stay) | First systematic dataset the farm has. Patterns visible immediately |
| Direct variety comparison results (cupping scores + fermentation data) | "Bourbon scored 86.5 washed, Caturra scored 85.8 washed — here's what differed in fermentation" |
| Process comparison results | "This Geisha scored 87 washed, 86 honey, 85.5 natural" — or whatever the data shows |
| Written protocols and templates | The farm can continue collecting data at this level of detail without her |
| Fermentation monitoring practice | pH/temp logging as habit, not experiment. Process control foundation |
| Microbial samples in transit to a lab | Results come back in weeks/months — the farm gets its microbial fingerprint |

### For her career

| Deliverable | Value |
|-------------|-------|
| A unique dataset | Controlled variety-comparison fermentation data from a Colombian farm. Doesn't exist in the literature |
| Field research experience | Bridges lab science and agricultural practice — very attractive for food science / pharmacognosy programs |
| Potential publication | If microbial sequencing shows variety-dependent community differences, that's a novel finding. Even negative results ("no significant difference under these conditions") are publishable |
| Colombian coffee research network | Contacts at Cenicafé, local universities, farm relationships. This network compounds over years |
| A research narrative | "I designed and executed a controlled fermentation study at a specialty farm in Huila, Colombia, comparing microbial community dynamics across arabica varieties" — that's a thesis chapter, a grant application paragraph, or a job interview story |
| Months of follow-up work | Sequencing analysis, data analysis, writing. The 6 weeks of field work generates 6+ months of intellectual output |

## What's NOT realistic in 6 weeks

- **Getting sequencing results back.** 16S/ITS turnaround is typically 2–8 weeks after submission, plus bioinformatics analysis. She collects the samples now; results come later.
- **HPLC compound tracking.** Setting up analytical chemistry from scratch requires lab access and instrument time she probably can't arrange in this window. This is a follow-up study.
- **Statistical significance from a single mitaca.** The mitaca is a smaller harvest. She might get 4–8 experimental lots. That's enough for preliminary findings and pilot data, not definitive proof.
- **Publication before she leaves.** The paper comes later. The 6 weeks produce the raw material.

This is fine. The point is to collect data that can't be collected remotely. Everything else — analysis, writing, publication — can happen from anywhere.

## Equipment checklist

**Likely already at the farm:**
- Scale (cherry and green bean weights)
- Moisture meter
- Roest sample roaster
- Cupping setup
- Fermentation tanks

**May need to acquire (~$100–200 total):**
- Digital pH meter with calibration solutions (~$30–50)
- Refractometer for Brix (~$20–30, may already have one)
- Digital thermometer with probe (~$15)
- Hygrometer for drying area (~$15)

**For microbial sampling (~$50–100 for supplies):**
- Sterile swabs or 15ml collection tubes
- Labels and permanent marker
- Cooler with ice packs for cold chain to lab
- 70% ethanol for sterilization

## Minimum viable version

If lab partnerships don't materialize in time, or if the mitaca harvest is light, the minimum valuable outcome is:

1. **Structured lot tracking** for every lot the farm processes during her stay
2. **pH and temperature logs** for every fermentation
3. **Split-process experiment** on at least one variety (washed vs. natural)
4. **Full SCA cupping** of all lots
5. **Written protocols** the farm can continue using

No lab needed. No special equipment beyond a pH meter. This alone gives Nogales more data about their own process than most specialty farms have, and gives her a dataset to analyze and write about.

The microbial sampling and sequencing are the high-upside addition, but the baseline is valuable on its own.
