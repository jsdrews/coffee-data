## Harvest timing at Bruselas

NASA POWER rainfall data for Bruselas (1.85°N, 76.08°W) shows a different pattern from the commonly cited "Huila main harvest = Oct–Dec":

```
Monthly rainfall at Bruselas, Huila (2018–2025 avg):
  Jan:   100 mm          Jul:   64 mm  ← DRY
  Feb:   114 mm          Aug:   35 mm  ← DRIEST
  Mar:   161 mm          Sep:   62 mm  ← DRY
  Apr:   171 mm  ← PEAK  Oct:  122 mm
  May:   129 mm          Nov:  158 mm
  Jun:   115 mm          Dec:  134 mm
```

Coffee flowering is triggered by rain returning after a dry period. At Bruselas:
- **Primary dry spell:** July–August (35–64 mm)
- **Rains return:** September–October → triggers primary flowering
- **Cherry development:** ~7–8 months at ~1800m
- **Estimated primary harvest: approximately April–June**

Southern Huila (Bruselas, Pitalito, Acevedo) often has its primary harvest April–June, with a secondary harvest October–December — the inverse of central/northern Colombia.

**Confirm with the farm.** This model is based on regional climate averages. Nogales' specific elevation, microclimate, and variety mix determine the actual dates. The first thing to establish is: when do cherries start coming in, and how long does the harvest window last?

Regardless of which option applies, the first week is the same.

**Deliverable:** Experimental design document — which varieties, how many lots, what comparisons, what measurements at each stage.

## Option A: Leave mid-May (~6 weeks)

**Timeline: April 3 – May 15, 2026**

Best case: cherry available by mid-April, giving ~4 weeks of hands-on cherry work. Worst case: cherry arrives late April, giving ~2.5 weeks. Either way, Option A is a focused sprint that produces pilot data and sets up the farm for long-term data collection.

### Phase 1: Setup (April 3–16)

Week 1 is shared setup (above). If cherry is already available, begin experiments immediately in week 2. If not, use week 2 to:

- Finalize lab partnership and confirm sample logistics
- Map the farm: GPS each parcel, photograph processing infrastructure, document elevation differences
- Collect soil samples from each parcel (send to a lab for pH, minerals, organic matter)
- Study the reference data (analysis notebook, compound profiles, variety characteristics)
- Prepare all equipment and sampling materials

### Phase 2: Fermentation experiments (as soon as cherry is available)

**Experiment 1 — Variety comparison:**
- Harvest 2–3 varieties on the same day (e.g., Geisha + Bourbon, or whatever is ripe)
- Process all with identical washed anaerobic fermentation
- Log pH, temperature, and Brix every 4–6 hours
- Collect microbial swabs at 0h, 24h, 48h, and end of fermentation
- Label everything: variety, parcel, date, timepoint, sample ID

**Experiment 2 — Process comparison:**
- Take one variety and split into 3 lots: washed, honey, natural
- Same monitoring protocol
- Directly answers: "does this variety score higher washed or natural?"

**Experiment 3 — Terroir comparison (if possible):**
- Same variety from 2 different parcels (different elevation or soil type)
- Identical processing
- Tests whether location affects fermentation microbiology

**Ongoing:** Track ALL lots the farm processes, not just experimental ones.

### Phase 3: Drying, roasting, cupping (if time allows)

If cherry arrived by mid-April:
- First lots finish drying by early May (~10–18 days)
- Roast on the Roest with full profile logging
- Cup using SCA protocol with 2+ people
- Full pipeline connected for at least some lots

If cherry arrived late April:
- Lots are still drying when she leaves
- **Hand off to the farm team:** drying monitoring, roasting, and cupping protocols are documented. The farm continues. She receives data remotely.

### Phase 4: Handoff (final 3–5 days)

**For the farm:**
- Clean compiled dataset of every lot tracked
- Written protocols for fermentation monitoring, lot tracking, drying logs, roast-to-lot linking
- Templates the farm team can continue using without her
- Preliminary findings from any completed lots

**For the research:**
- Ship microbial samples to the lab (if not already done)
- Compile raw data into structured format
- Write up: experimental design, methods, preliminary observations
- Plan for remote follow-up when sequencing results and cupping data arrive

### What Option A produces

| Output | Depends on cherry timing |
|--------|-------------------------|
| Structured lot tracking data for all lots during her stay | No — valuable regardless |
| Fermentation curves (pH, temp, Brix) for 4–8 experimental lots | Needs cherry, but even 2 weeks is enough |
| Microbial samples (8–16 samples) shipped to lab | Needs cherry + lab partnership |
| Soil samples from each parcel | No cherry needed |
| Farm map (parcels, varieties, elevations, GPS) | No cherry needed |
| Full pipeline data (cherry → cup) for some lots | Only if cherry arrives by mid-April |
| Protocols and templates for the farm to continue | No cherry needed |

### What Option A does NOT produce

- Sequencing results (2–8 weeks turnaround after submission)
- HPLC compound tracking (needs lab access she can't set up in this window)
- Statistical significance (4–8 lots = pilot data, not proof)
- A publication (the paper comes later; 6 weeks produces the raw material)
- Full-pipeline data if cherry arrives late (farm completes drying/roasting/cupping after she leaves)

## Option B: Stay through the full harvest (~3 months)

**Timeline: April 3 – late June 2026 (assuming harvest runs April–June)**

This changes the research from a pilot study to a substantive dataset. Three months of continuous presence during the primary harvest means higher sample sizes, replication, complete pipeline data, and time for the slower parts of the process (drying, roasting iteration, cupping calibration).

### Phase 1: Setup and early harvest (April 3–20)

Same as Option A week 1–2, but with more time to be thorough:
- Complete farm mapping (GPS, soil sampling, weather logger installation)
- Establish lab partnerships with time for back-and-forth
- Begin lot tracking as soon as first cherry arrives
- Run first experimental fermentations

### Phase 2: Core experiments (April 21 – May 31)

This is the heart of the harvest. With 5–6 weeks of continuous cherry access:

**Experiment 1 — Variety comparison (expanded):**
- Compare 3–5 varieties (not just 2) under identical fermentation
- Run **replicate fermentations** — same variety, same process, different days
- Replication is what separates a pilot from publishable data
- Target: 3 varieties × 2–3 replicates = 6–9 fermentation runs
- Microbial sampling at each: 4 timepoints × 6–9 runs = 24–36 samples

**Experiment 2 — Process comparison (expanded):**
- 2–3 varieties × 3 processing methods (washed, honey, natural) = 6–9 lots
- Full monitoring on each
- With replication: enough data to see whether variety × process interaction matters (does Geisha respond differently to natural processing than Bourbon does?)

**Experiment 3 — Terroir comparison:**
- Same variety from different parcels, replicated across multiple harvest days
- Soil microbiome samples paired with fermentation microbiome samples

**Experiment 4 — Fermentation duration series (time permitting):**
- Same variety, same process, but pull lots at different endpoints (48h, 72h, 96h, 120h)
- Tests whether there's an optimal fermentation duration for this variety at this farm
- pH/temp curves show when microbial activity plateaus — is that the right stopping point?

**Ongoing throughout:**
- Structured lot tracking for ALL farm lots (not just experiments)
- Drying monitoring for all lots in progress
- Regular equipment calibration (pH meter drift is real)

### Phase 3: Roasting and cupping (May 15 – June 15)

With a 3-month timeline, early lots finish drying while later lots are still fermenting. This means she can:

- **Roast completed lots on the Roest** with full profile logging
- **Iterate on roast profiles** — try 2–3 profiles per green lot to see how roast level interacts with variety and process
- **Cup systematically** — enough lots to compare variety × process × roast level
- **Calibrate cupping** — cup with the farm team over multiple sessions to build shared vocabulary and consistency
- **Connect the full pipeline** for 15–25+ lots: cherry Brix → fermentation curve → drying profile → roast profile → SCA score
- **Begin preliminary analysis** — which variables correlate with cupping score? Do the fermentation curves predict anything?

### Phase 4: Advanced work (June 1–25)

With early experiments completed and cupped, she has time for:

1. **Second-round experiments** informed by first-round results
   - If the variety comparison showed a clear difference, test a new variety
   - If fermentation duration mattered, narrow the range
   - If a specific process produced surprising results, replicate to confirm

2. **HPLC sampling** (if lab partnership allows)
   - With 3 months, there's time to arrange HPLC access at a university
   - Collect samples at 4 stages: fresh cherry → post-fermentation → green → roasted
   - Even 8–12 HPLC samples across 2–3 lots would provide compound fate data
   - This is the data that connects the fermentation work to the pharmacognosy angle

3. **Data compilation and preliminary analysis**
   - Full dataset assembled and cleaned
   - Preliminary statistical analysis (do varieties differ significantly? does process matter?)
   - Draft figures and tables for a potential publication
   - Identify which findings are strongest and worth writing up first

### Phase 5: Handoff (final week)

Same deliverables as Option A, but substantially richer:

**For the farm:**
- 3 months of structured lot data (potentially 30–50+ lots tracked)
- Tested, refined protocols (not just written — actually used and iterated on)
- Cupping data linked to processing data for every experimental lot
- Concrete findings: "variety X scored Y when processed Z; here's what the fermentation curves show"
- Recommendations for the secondary harvest (Oct–Dec): which experiments to repeat, what to change

**For the research:**
- Complete dataset with replication
- 24–36+ microbial samples (enough for statistical comparison)
- Full pipeline data for 15–25+ lots
- Possibly HPLC compound data for a subset
- Draft manuscript outline with preliminary figures
- Strong basis for a thesis chapter or publication

### What Option B produces that Option A cannot

| Output | Option A (6 weeks) | Option B (3 months) |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------|
| Experimental lots | 4–8 | 15–25+ |
| Microbial samples | 8–16 | 24–36+ |
| Replicated experiments | Unlikely | Yes — 2–3 replicates per condition |
| Full pipeline data (cherry → cup) | Maybe some lots | Most lots |
| Roast profile iteration | Limited | Multiple profiles per green |
| Cupping calibration | 1–2 sessions | Multiple sessions, refined |
| HPLC compound tracking | Not feasible | Possible with lab partnership |
| Statistical power | Pilot data | Approaching publishable |
| Second-round experiments | No time | Informed by first-round results |
| Preliminary manuscript | No | Draft outline + figures |

### The compounding effect

The difference between Option A and Option B isn't just "more of the same." Three months creates a qualitative shift:

- **Replication.** A single variety comparison is an anecdote. Three replicates of the same comparison is data. Reviewers and journals care about this distinction.
- **Iteration.** First-round experiments reveal what's interesting. Second-round experiments test whether it's real. Six weeks only allows the first round.
- **Complete pipelines.** With 3 months, she personally controls every step from cherry to cup for most lots. With 6 weeks, the farm finishes some lots after she leaves — introducing process variation she can't account for.
- **Deeper relationships.** Three months at a farm builds trust and understanding that 6 weeks cannot. The farm team becomes invested in the research. Lab partners have time to engage meaningfully. This is what turns a one-time project into an ongoing collaboration.
- **Career differentiation.** "I spent 6 weeks at a Colombian farm" is a good line. "I designed and ran a 3-month controlled study across the full primary harvest" is a fundamentally different credential.
