# BMEI Biotechnology Program — Summary

Summary of three documents from the BMEI (Bio-ME Integradas) microbial inoculation project at Nogales, presented by **Weimar Obando Gómez** (Agronomist, M.Sc candidate, Weimagro SAS).

## What BMEI is

BMEI is a **bioelectrochemical system for producing agricultural bioinputs** — living microbial products that replace or supplement chemical fertilizers, fungicides, and pesticides. The system multiplies commercially sourced microbial strains in 600L tanks under semi-controlled conditions (temperature, pH, aeration, electromagnetic treatment), then applies them to soil and foliage.

The core idea: instead of buying chemical inputs, grow your own biological inputs on-farm using a standardized "bioplant" (a small production facility with tanks, aeration systems, and monitoring equipment).

## The three bioinput formulations

### BioM-1: Biofertility and nutrient solubilization

**Purpose:** Improve soil fertility, degrade organic residues (pulp, husks, parchment), increase microbial activity.

**Organisms:**
| Microbe | Function |
|---------|----------|
| *Azospirillum brasilense* | Nitrogen fixation, auxin production (stimulates roots) |
| *Azotobacter chroococcum* | Free nitrogen fixation, increases organic matter |
| *Pseudomonas fluorescens* | Phosphorus solubilization, disease suppression |
| *Bacillus pumilus* | Plant growth promotion, stress tolerance |
| *Bacillus megaterium* | Phosphorus solubilization |
| *Penicillium janthinellum* | Phosphorus solubilization from rock phosphate |
| *Lactobacillus acidophilus* | Lactic acid fermentation, pathogen reduction |
| *Saccharomyces cerevisiae* | Rapid fermentation, B-vitamin production |

**Recipe (600L):** 2L inoculum (Digestor) + 10L molasses + 2kg soy milk + 1L Isabion (amino acids) + water. Aerated on 1hr on/off cycles.

**Application:** 20–30L per plant per year, split into 6 applications. Applied to the root zone and over organic residues.

### BioM-2: Biological disease control

**Purpose:** Preventive and suppressive control of coffee diseases.

**Target diseases:**
- Coffee leaf rust (*Hemileia vastatrix*) — the #1 disease threat to Arabica worldwide
- Dieback (*Phoma* sp.)
- American leaf spot / "ojo de gallo" (*Mycena citricolor*)
- Anthracnose (*Colletotrichum* spp.)

**Organisms:**
| Microbe | Product | Function |
|---------|---------|----------|
| *Bacillus amyloliquefaciens* QST713 | Serenade | Produces lipopeptide antifungals (iturins, surfactins, fengycins) |
| *Bacillus subtilis* DSM 5750 | Alicerce | Biofilm formation on leaves/roots, intense antibiotic production |
| *Bacillus licheniformis* DSM 5749 | Alicerce | Extracellular enzymes, organic matter degradation |

**Key mechanism:** These Bacillus strains produce **natural antibiotics** (lipopeptides) that directly inhibit fungal pathogens. They also form protective biofilms on root and leaf surfaces, physically occupying the ecological niche before pathogens can colonize.

**Application:** 10–15L per plant at disease hotspots. Monthly during high-humidity months.

### BioM-3: Biological insect control

**Purpose:** Control of coffee pests without chemical pesticides.

**Target pests:**
- Coffee berry borer / broca (*Hypothenemus hampei*) — the #1 insect pest in coffee
- Mites (*Tetranychus* spp.)
- Fruit flies
- *Monalonion velezangeli* (chamusquina — a mirid bug)

**Organisms:**
| Microbe | Function |
|---------|----------|
| *Metarhizium anisopliae* | Entomopathogenic fungus — infects and kills thrips, mites |
| *Beauveria bassiana* | Entomopathogenic fungus — kills chewing and sucking insects including broca |
| *Bacillus thuringiensis* var. kurstaki | Produces toxins against defoliating larvae |
| *Bacillus popilliae* | Targets beetle larvae (relevant to broca, which is a beetle) |
| *Lecanicillium lecanii* | Entomopathogenic fungus — whiteflies, aphids, mites |
| *Paecilomyces fumosoroseus* | Entomopathogenic fungus — broad spectrum |
| *Methylobacterium symbioticum* | Nitrogen-fixing methylotroph |

**Application:** Foliar spray, diluted 1:5 with water. Applied every 20–30 days depending on pest pressure.

## The production process (BMEI phases)

1. **Selection** — Choose microbial strains based on agronomic need (which diseases, which pests, which nutrient deficiencies)
2. **Adaptation** — Customize the base inoculum for the specific application (insecticide vs. fungicide vs. biofertilizer)
3. **Incubation & multiplication** — Fermentation in 600L tanks with aeration, molasses as energy source, soy protein for amino acids
4. **Stabilization** — Hydrodynamic and fluid mechanics processing
5. **Electromagnetic ionization** — BMEI's proprietary step: applying magnetic fields (10–20 gauss) to the microbial mixture. Claimed to improve ionic orientation, nutrient mobility, and metabolite stability
6. **Mineralization & chelation** — Binding minerals to organic carriers for plant availability
7. **Storage** — In shade, avoid temperature extremes, use immediately after preparation
8. **Certification** — Regulatory compliance

### Her open questions (answered)

**"What are organolépticas?"**
Organoleptic properties = sensory characteristics. In coffee: aroma, flavor, acidity, body, aftertaste — what SCA cupping measures. The BMEI program aims to influence organoleptic quality through soil health.

**"Is Trichoderma being 'aggressive' good or bad?"**
Good. Trichoderma is an **aggressive colonizer** of soil space — it grows fast and occupies ecological niches before pathogens can. It's also a mycoparasite (attacks other fungi). "Aggressive" in this context means competitively dominant, which is exactly what you want for biological disease control.

**"Is Bacillus beneficial or harmful?"**
Beneficial. The Bacillus species in the BMEI formulations (*B. subtilis*, *B. amyloliquefaciens*, *B. licheniformis*, *B. pumilus*, *B. megaterium*, *B. thuringiensis*, *B. popilliae*) are all well-documented beneficial soil/plant bacteria. They produce antibiotics, solubilize nutrients, form protective biofilms, and kill insect pests. Bacillus is one of the most studied and widely used genera in agricultural biocontrol.

**"Surface = microbial growth, liquid body = metabolites?"**
Approximately yes. In a static or semi-aerated tank:
- **Surface/biofilm:** Higher oxygen = more aerobic growth, active cell division, biofilm matrix production. This is where you'd see the highest cell density.
- **Liquid body:** Lower oxygen = metabolite accumulation. As cells grow, they consume sugars and produce metabolites (organic acids, lipopeptides, enzymes). These diffuse into the liquid.
- **Bottom:** Settled cells, dead cells, precipitates. May have higher concentration of spores (Bacillus sporulates under stress).

Stratified sampling (surface, middle, bottom) is the right approach.

**"Optical density vs. PPM — which is more appropriate?"**
Optical density (OD600) is the standard method for tracking microbial growth in liquid culture. It measures how cloudy the liquid is — more cells = more cloudiness. Cheap, fast, requires only a spectrophotometer. PPM likely refers to dissolved solids or specific mineral concentrations — useful for tracking nutrient depletion but not growth directly.

**Best approach with available equipment:** pH + Brix + visual observation (color, smell, foam/biofilm formation) at regular intervals. If she can access a spectrophotometer, add OD600. These four measurements together give a good picture of growth dynamics without expensive equipment.

**"How to reconcile 15-day batches vs. 3/6/9 month monitoring?"**
These are two different scales:
- **Per-batch monitoring (Day 0, 1, 2, 3, 11):** Tracks growth within a single fermentation cycle. "Did this batch grow properly? Is it ready to apply?"
- **Longitudinal monitoring (3, 6, 9 months):** Tracks the bioplant system over time. "Is the inoculum still viable after storage? Are results consistent across batches? Is the tank infrastructure developing persistent contamination?"

Both are needed. Per-batch is quality control. Longitudinal is process validation.

## Notes on the electromagnetic ionization claim

The BMEI process includes an "electromagnetic ionization" step (10–20 gauss magnetic fields applied to the microbial mixtures). This is presented as improving ionic orientation, nutrient mobility, and metabolite stability.

This claim should be treated with caution. While there is some research on magnetic field effects on microbial growth and nutrient solubility, the evidence base is limited and the proposed mechanisms are not well-established in mainstream microbiology. The biological components of the BMEI system (the microbial strains, the nutrient formulations, the fermentation process) are well-supported by science. The electromagnetic component is the least substantiated part.

This doesn't invalidate the overall system — the microbes themselves are proven biocontrol and biofertilization agents regardless of whether magnetic treatment adds value.
